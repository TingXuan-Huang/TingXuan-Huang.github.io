I re-read *Notes from Underground* last week. The first time I read it, I was nineteen, and I mostly remember thinking the narrator was insufferable. He still is. But I think I was missing the point.

The narrator of *Notes* is not, primarily, a man complaining about the world. He is a man complaining about his own complaining. He is aware that he is unreasonable. He is aware that his awareness of being unreasonable does not make him less unreasonable. He is aware of *that*, too, and resents being made to notice.

Early in the book, he writes:

> I swear, gentlemen, that to be too conscious is an illness — a real thoroughgoing illness. For man's everyday needs, it would have been quite enough to have the ordinary human consciousness, that is, half or a quarter of the amount which falls to the lot of a cultivated man of our unhappy nineteenth century.

I have been turning this over for a few days. The temptation is to read it as ironic — as Dostoevsky distancing himself from his pathetic narrator. And it is, partly. But there's a more uncomfortable reading: that Dostoevsky meant it.

## Consciousness as friction

Most of the unhappy hours of my life, looked at honestly, have been spent watching myself be unhappy. Not the original unhappiness — that part is usually short. The second layer, where I observe myself failing to handle the first layer well, is where the real time goes.

The Underground Man has the same problem, taken to its logical extreme. He cannot make a decision, because every decision is immediately attended by a small chorus of internal commentators pointing out what's wrong with it. He cannot act spitefully, because he can see that the spite is petty. He cannot act kindly, because he suspects himself of performing kindness for the audience in his own head.

What Dostoevsky is doing, I think, is putting a *specific kind of intelligence* on trial. Not intelligence about the world — the Underground Man is very smart about the world. Intelligence about oneself, applied without rest, in the absence of any practice that could metabolize it.

## A small practical conclusion

I am not going to stop being conscious. I am not going to stop noticing what I notice. But I have become more suspicious of the third and fourth layers — the ones where I notice that I noticed, and then have opinions about those opinions.

When I catch myself there now, I try to do something with my hands. Wash a dish. Pet the cat. Go outside. Anything that breaks the loop.

The Underground Man did not have a cat. I think this matters more than it sounds.
