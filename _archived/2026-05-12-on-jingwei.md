There's a Chinese myth I keep returning to. A girl named Nüwa drowns in the Eastern Sea, and her soul becomes a small bird called Jingwei. The bird spends the rest of its days carrying pebbles and twigs from the western mountains to the sea, one beakful at a time, trying to fill it in.

It is, on its face, an absurd project. The sea is the sea. The bird is the bird. The pebbles are not large.

I used to read this story as a tragedy of futile effort — the kind of thing a Stoic might admire and a productivity coach would gently redirect. Lately I've been reading it differently. Jingwei is not really trying to fill the sea. Jingwei is trying to keep moving, in the direction of something that mattered enough to die for.

## Practice as direction, not destination

A lot of what I do — running simulations, debugging models, writing notes nobody reads — does not really *finish*. There is no last pebble. The sea of things I'd like to understand is, by any reasonable measure, infinite. What I have instead is a direction, and the small daily decision to keep flying west and then east and then west again.

It helps to remember that the bird in the story is not stupid. The bird knows. The bird flies anyway.

## What this changes

When I started thinking about practice this way — as a direction maintained, not a sea drained — a few small things shifted:

- I stopped measuring days by whether I "made progress." Most days I did not, in any way I could see.
- I started measuring them by whether I had pointed myself the right way and moved.
- I got less attached to the idea that the work would, at some point, *be done*.

None of this is original. Every contemplative tradition has some version of it. What was new for me was attaching the idea to a small angry bird, instead of to a meditation cushion.

I find that I take instruction better from birds.
