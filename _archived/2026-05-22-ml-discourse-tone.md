Reading the last few weeks of takes on a single capability paper, I keep noticing a pattern. The researchers describe what they actually found — three benchmarks, two ablations, a known weakness on a fourth. The commentators describe what it would *mean* if the result generalized — a sea change, or a fizzle, depending on prior. The two paragraphs sit next to each other on the same feed as if they're the same kind of statement.

They aren't. One is a careful claim about a measured artifact. The other is a forecast about a counterfactual. I don't think the gap is bad faith. I think it's a genre difference — researchers write about what's true *now*, commentators write about what could be true *next*, and the discourse mostly hasn't found a clean grammar for translating between them.

What I want, and don't yet have a recipe for, is the habit of reading both with the right kind of patience. The paper deserves to be read as evidence. The commentary deserves to be read as *speculation about consequences of evidence*. The error is collapsing one into the other.
